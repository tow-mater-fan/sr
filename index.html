// 01 — FOUNDATIONS

What is Solubility?

When an ionic compound dissolves in water, it breaks apart into its cations (+) and anions (−), surrounded by water molecules.

The dissolving process

Take table salt — NaCl. When it dissolves:

NaCl(s) → Na⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq)

The (s) means solid, and (aq) means aqueous (dissolved in water). When a compound does NOT dissolve, we use (s) as the product too.

Solubility definitions

Term Meaning Amount dissolved at 25°C
S Soluble Dissolves significantly in water > 1 g per 100 mL water
P Slightly soluble Small amount dissolves 0.1 – 1 g per 100 mL
I Insoluble Does not dissolve meaningfully < 0.1 g per 100 mL
⚡

Important: "Insoluble" doesn't mean zero dissolves — it means so little that for most purposes we treat it as zero.

Why does it matter?

When two solutions are mixed, sometimes a solid forms — called a precipitate. Predicting whether a precipitate forms is critical in:

Water treatment Drug synthesis Environmental science Qualitative analysis Industrial chemistry
// 02 — THE RULES

The 7 Solubility Rules

These rules let you predict solubility for any ionic compound. Click each rule to expand it.

💡

Memory tip: Start with the "always soluble" rules, then work through the exceptions. If no rule applies, assume insoluble.

Quick Reference Table

Ion / Group Result Exceptions
NO₃⁻ (nitrate), CH₃COO⁻ (acetate) SOLUBLE None
Group 1 ions (Li⁺, Na⁺, K⁺, Rb⁺, Cs⁺), NH₄⁺ SOLUBLE None
Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻ (halides) SOLUBLE Ag⁺, Pb²⁺, Hg₂²⁺ form insoluble precipitates
SO₄²⁻ (sulfate) SOLUBLE Ba²⁺, Pb²⁺, Ca²⁺, Sr²⁺ form insoluble sulfates
OH⁻ (hydroxide) INSOLUBLE Group 1 + Ba²⁺ are soluble; Ca²⁺, Sr²⁺ slightly soluble
CO₃²⁻, PO₄³⁻, CrO₄²⁻, S²⁻ INSOLUBLE Group 1 + NH₄⁺ are always soluble
Most other compounds INSOLUBLE Use above rules first
